
STATISTICAL ANALYSIS SUMMARY

This study examined the effects of negative feedback on bag area fixation patterns in an eye-tracking experiment. 
Two groups were compared: Group 1 (with negative feedback) and Group 2 (without negative feedback), each 
consisting of two participants.

DESCRIPTIVE STATISTICS:

Group 1 (Negative Feedback): 189 bag fixations, 
mean duration = 212.8ms (SD = 231.8ms), 
total duration = 40,227ms

Group 2 (No Negative Feedback): 128 bag fixations, 
mean duration = 244.1ms (SD = 173.7ms), 
total duration = 31,241ms

NORMALITY ASSESSMENT:
Shapiro-Wilk tests were conducted to assess normality of fixation durations. 
Group 1: W = 0.412, p = 0.0000 
Group 2: W = 0.741, p = 0.0000

Both groups did not follow 
normal distributions (α = 0.05).

INDEPENDENT T-TEST:
An independent t-test was conducted to compare bag fixation durations between groups.
t(315) = -1.293, 
p = 0.1969

The difference in bag fixation durations between groups was 
not statistically significant (α = 0.05).

Effect size (Cohen's d) = -0.148, indicating a negligible effect.

MANN-WHITNEY U TEST:
A non-parametric Mann-Whitney U test was also conducted as a robust alternative.
U = 9902.000, p = 0.0061

The non-parametric test confirmed 
the parametric test results (α = 0.05).

Effect size (r) = 0.181, indicating a small effect.

CHI-SQUARE TEST:
A chi-square test of independence was conducted to examine the association between 
experimental group and bag area fixation frequency.
χ²(1) = 15.866, p = 0.0001

The association between experimental group and bag area fixation frequency was 
statistically significant (α = 0.05).

Effect size (Cramer's V) = 0.142, indicating a small effect.

CONCLUSION:

The results did not reveal statistically significant differences in bag area fixation 
patterns between the experimental groups. However, the effect sizes suggest 
negligible practical differences that may warrant further investigation with larger sample sizes.


LIMITATIONS:
- Small sample size (n = 2 per group) limits statistical power
- Individual differences may confound group effects
- Limited generalizability due to specific experimental conditions

FUTURE DIRECTIONS:
- Replicate with larger sample sizes
- Include additional eye-tracking metrics (saccade patterns, scan paths)
- Investigate individual differences and moderating factors
